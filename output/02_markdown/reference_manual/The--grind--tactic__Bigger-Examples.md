#  16.12. Bigger Examples[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=grind-bigger-examples "Permalink")
##  16.12.1. Integrating `grind`'s Features[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=The-Lean-Language-Reference--The--grind--tactic--Bigger-Examples--Integrating--grind-___s-Features "Permalink")
This example demonstrates how the various submodules of `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")` are seamlessly integrated. In particular we can:
  * instantiate theorems from the library, using custom patterns,
  * perform case splitting,
  * do linear integer arithmetic reasoning, including modularity conditions, and
  * do Gröbner basis reasoning all without providing explicit instructions to drive the interactions between these modes of reasoning.


For this example we'll begin with a “mocked up” version of the real numbers, and the `sin` and `cos` functions. Of course, this example works [without any changes](https://github.com/leanprover-community/mathlib4/blob/master/MathlibTest/grind/trig.lean) using Mathlib's versions of these!
`[axiom](Axioms/#Lean___Parser___Command___axiom-next "Documentation for syntax") R : Type   @[[instance](Type-Classes/Instance-Declarations/#Lean___Parser___Attr___instance "Documentation for syntax")] [axiom](Axioms/#Lean___Parser___Command___axiom-next "Documentation for syntax") instCommRingR : [Lean.Grind.CommRing](The--grind--tactic/Algebraic-Solver-_LPAR_Commutative-Rings___-Fields_RPAR_/#Lean___Grind___CommRing___mk "Documentation for Lean.Grind.CommRing") [R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example")   [axiom](Axioms/#Lean___Parser___Command___axiom-next "Documentation for syntax") sin : [R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example") → [R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example") [axiom](Axioms/#Lean___Parser___Command___axiom-next "Documentation for syntax") cos : [R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example") → [R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example") [axiom](Axioms/#Lean___Parser___Command___axiom-next "Documentation for syntax") trig_identity : ∀ x, ([cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x)^2 + ([sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x)^2 = 1 `
Our first step is to tell grind to “put the trig identity on the whiteboard” whenever it sees a goal involving `[sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example")` or `[cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example")`:
`grind_pattern [trig_identity](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#trig_identity "Definition of example") => [cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x grind_pattern [trig_identity](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#trig_identity "Definition of example") => [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x `
Note here we use **two** different patterns for the same theorem, so the theorem is instantiated even if `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")` sees just one of these functions. If we preferred to more conservatively instantiate the theorem only when both `[sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example")` and `[cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example")` are present, we could have used a multi-pattern:
`grind_pattern [trig_identity](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#trig_identity "Definition of example") => [cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x, [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x `
For this example, either approach will work.
Because `grind` immediately notices the trig identity, we can prove goals like this:
`example : ([cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x + [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x)^2 = 2 * [cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x * [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x + 1 := byx:[R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example")⊢ [(](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd")[cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x [+](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd") [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x[)](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd") [^](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HPow___mk "Documentation for HPow.hPow") 2 [=](Basic-Propositions/Propositional-Equality/#Eq___refl "Documentation for Eq") 2 [*](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HMul___mk "Documentation for HMul.hMul") [cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x [*](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HMul___mk "Documentation for HMul.hMul") [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x [+](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd") 1   [grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")All goals completed! 🐙 `
Here `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")` does the following:
  1. It notices `[cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x` and `[sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x`, so instantiates the trig identity.
  2. It notices that this is a polynomial in `[CommRing](The--grind--tactic/Algebraic-Solver-_LPAR_Commutative-Rings___-Fields_RPAR_/#Lean___Grind___CommRing___mk "Documentation for Lean.Grind.CommRing") [R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example")`, and sends it to the Gröbner basis module. No calculation happens at this point: it's the first polynomial relation in this ring, so the Gröbner basis is updated to `[([cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x)^2 + ([sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x)^2 - 1]`.
  3. It notices that the left and right hand sides of the goal are polynomials in `[CommRing](The--grind--tactic/Algebraic-Solver-_LPAR_Commutative-Rings___-Fields_RPAR_/#Lean___Grind___CommRing___mk "Documentation for Lean.Grind.CommRing") [R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example")`, and sends them to the Gröbner basis module for normalization.


Since their normal forms modulo `([cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x)^2 + ([sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x)^2 = 1` are equal, their equivalence classes are merged, and the goal is solved.
We can also do this sort of argument when [congruence closure](The--grind--tactic/Congruence-Closure/#--tech-term-Congruence-closure) is needed:
`example (f : [R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example") → [Nat](Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat")) :     f (([cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x + [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x)^2) = f (2 * [cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x * [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x + 1) := byx:[R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example")f:[R](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#R "Definition of example") → [Nat](Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat")⊢ f [(](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HPow___mk "Documentation for HPow.hPow")[(](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd")[cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x [+](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd") [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x[)](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd") [^](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HPow___mk "Documentation for HPow.hPow") 2[)](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HPow___mk "Documentation for HPow.hPow") [=](Basic-Propositions/Propositional-Equality/#Eq___refl "Documentation for Eq") f [(](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd")2 [*](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HMul___mk "Documentation for HMul.hMul") [cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x [*](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HMul___mk "Documentation for HMul.hMul") [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x [+](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd") 1[)](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#HAdd___mk "Documentation for HAdd.hAdd")   [grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")All goals completed! 🐙 `
As before, `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")` instantiates the trig identity, notices that `([cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x + [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x)^2` and `2 * [cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x * [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x + 1` are equal modulo `([cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x)^2 + ([sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x)^2 = 1`, puts those algebraic expressions in the same equivalence class, and then puts the function applications `f (([cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x + [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x)^2)` and `f (2 * [cos](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#cos "Definition of example") x * [sin](The--grind--tactic/Bigger-Examples/#sin "Definition of example") x + 1)` in the same equivalence class, and closes the goal.

As before, `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")` first does the instantiation and Gröbner basis calculations required to identify the two function applications. However the `cutsat` algorithm by itself can't do anything with `[max](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#Max___mk "Documentation for Max.max") 3 (4 * n) ≠ 2 + n`. Next, after instantiating `Nat.max_def` (automatically, because of an annotation in the standard library) which states `∀ {n m : [Nat](Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat")}, [max](Type-Classes/Basic-Classes/#Max___mk "Documentation for Max.max") n m = [if](Terms/Conditionals/#termIfThenElse "Documentation for syntax") n ≤ m [then](Terms/Conditionals/#termIfThenElse "Documentation for syntax") m [else](Terms/Conditionals/#termIfThenElse "Documentation for syntax") n`, `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")` can then case split on the inequality. In the branch `3 ≤ 4 * n`, `cutsat` again uses modularity to prove `4 * n ≠ 2 + n`. In the branch `4 * n < 3`, `cutsat` quickly determines `n = 0`, and then notices that `4 * 0 ≠ 2 + 0`.
This has been, of course, a quite artificial example! In practice, this sort of automatic integration of different reasoning modes is very powerful: the central “whiteboard” which tracks instantiated theorems and equivalence classes can hand off relevant terms and equalities to the appropriate modules (here, `cutsat` and Gröbner bases), which can then return new facts to the whiteboard.
##  16.12.2. `if`-`then`-`else` Normalization[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=grind-if-then-else-norm "Permalink")
This example is a showcase for the “out of the box” power of `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")`. Later examples will explore adding `[@[](Attributes/#Lean___Parser___Term___attributes-next "Documentation for syntax")grind[]](Attributes/#Lean___Parser___Term___attributes-next "Documentation for syntax")` annotations as part of the development process, to make `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")` more effective in a new domain. This example does not rely on any of the algebra extensions to `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")`, we're just using:
  * instantiation of annotated theorems from the library,
  * [congruence closure](The--grind--tactic/Congruence-Closure/#--tech-term-Congruence-closure), and
  * case splitting.


The solution here builds on an earlier formalization by Chris Hughes, but with some notable improvements:
  * the verification is separate from the code,
  * the proof is now a one-liner combining `[fun_induction](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#fun_induction "Documentation for tactic")` and `[grind](Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#grind "Documentation for tactic")`,
  * the proof is robust to changes in the code (e.g. swapping out `[HashMap](Basic-Types/Maps-and-Sets/#Std___HashMap "Documentation for Std.HashMap")` for `[TreeMap](Basic-Types/Maps-and-Sets/#Std___TreeMap "Documentation for Std.TreeMap")`) as well as changes to the precise verification conditions.


###  16.12.2.1. The problem[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=The-Lean-Language-Reference--The--grind--tactic--Bigger-Examples--if---then---else--Normalization--The-problem "Permalink")
Here is Rustan Leino's original description of the problem, as [posted by Leonardo de Moura](https://leanprover.zulipchat.com/#narrow/stream/113488-general/topic/Rustan's.20challenge) on the Lean Zulip:
> The data structure is an expression with Boolean literals, variables, and if-then-else expressions.
> The goal is to normalize such expressions into a form where: a) No nested ifs: the condition part of an if-expression is not itself an if-expression b) No constant tests: the condition part of an if-expression is not a constant c) No redundant ifs: the then and else branches of an if are not the same d) Each variable is evaluated at most once: the free variables of the condition are disjoint from those in the then branch, and also disjoint from those in the else branch.
> One should show that a normalization function produces an expression satisfying these four conditions, and one should also prove that the normalization function preserves the meaning of the given expression.
