#  Interlude: Tactics, Induction, and Proofs[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=tactics-induction-proofs "Permalink")
##  A Note on Proofs and User Interfaces[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=proofs-and-uis "Permalink")
This book presents the process of writing proofs as if they are written in one go and submitted to Lean, which then replies with error messages that describe what remains to be done. The actual process of interacting with Lean is much more pleasant. Lean provides information about the proof as the cursor is moved through it and there are a number of interactive features that make proving easier. Please consult the documentation of your Lean development environment for more information.
The approach in this book that focuses on incrementally building a proof and showing the messages that result demonstrates the kinds of interactive feedback that Lean provides while writing a proof, even though it is much slower than the process used by experts. At the same time, seeing incomplete proofs evolve towards completeness is a useful perspective on proving. As your skill in writing proofs increases, Lean's feedback will come to feel less like errors and more like support for your own thought processes. Learning the interactive approach is very important.
##  Recursion and Induction[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=recursion-vs-induction "Permalink")
The functions `plusR_succ_left` and `plusR_zero_left` from the preceding chapter can be seen from two perspectives. On the one hand, they are recursive functions that build up evidence for a proposition, just as other recursive functions might construct a list, a string, or any other data structure. On the other, they also correspond to proofs by _mathematical induction_.
Mathematical induction is a proof technique where a statement is proven for _all_ natural numbers in two steps:
  1. The statement is shown to hold for `000`. This is called the _base case_.
  2. Under the assumption that the statement holds for some arbitrarily chosen number `nnn`, it is shown to hold for `n+1n + 1n+1`. This is called the _induction step_. The assumption that the statement holds for `nnn` is called the _induction hypothesis_.


Because it's impossible to check the statement for _every_ natural number, induction provides a means of writing a proof that could, in principle, be expanded to any particular natural number. For example, if a concrete proof were desired for the number 3, then it could be constructed by using first the base case and then the induction step three times, to show the statement for 0, 1, 2, and finally 3. Thus, it proves the statement for all natural numbers.
##  The Induction Tactic[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=induction-tactic "Permalink")
Writing proofs by induction as recursive functions that use helpers such as `[congrArg](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Basic-Propositions/Propositional-Equality/#congrArg "Documentation for congrArg in Lean Language Reference")` does not always do a good job of expressing the intentions behind the proof. While recursive functions indeed have the structure of induction, they should probably be viewed as an _encoding_ of a proof. Furthermore, Lean's tactic system provides a number of opportunities to automate the construction of a proof that are not available when writing the recursive function explicitly. Lean provides an induction _tactic_ that can carry out an entire proof by induction in a single tactic block. Behind the scenes, Lean constructs the recursive function that corresponds the use of induction.

A tactic block is a program that is run while the Lean type checker processes a file, somewhat like a much more powerful C preprocessor macro. The tactics generate the actual program.
In the tactic language, there can be a number of goals. Each goal consists of a type together with some assumptions. These are analogous to using underscores as placeholders—the type in the goal represents what is to be proved, and the assumptions represent what is in-scope and can be used. In the case of the goal `case zero`, there are no assumptions and the type is `[Nat.zero](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat.zero in Lean Language Reference") = Nat.plusR 0 [Nat.zero](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat.zero in Lean Language Reference")`—this is the theorem statement with `0` instead of `k`. In the goal `case succ`, there are two assumptions, named `n✝` and `n_ih✝`. Behind the scenes, the `[induction](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#induction "Documentation for tactic in Lean Language Reference")` tactic creates a dependent pattern match that refines the overall type, and `n✝` represents the argument to `[Nat.succ](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat.succ in Lean Language Reference")` in the pattern. The assumption `n_ih✝` represents the result of calling the generated function recursively on `n✝`. Its type is the overall type of the theorem, just with `n✝` instead of `k`. The type to be fulfilled as part of the goal `case succ` is the overall theorem statement, with `Nat.succ n✝` instead of `k`.
The two goals that result from the use of the `[induction](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Tactic-Proofs/Tactic-Reference/#induction "Documentation for tactic in Lean Language Reference")` tactic correspond to the base case and the induction step in the description of mathematical induction. The base case is `case zero`. In `case succ`, `n_ih✝` corresponds to the induction hypothesis, while the whole of `case succ` is the induction step.

##  Tactic Golf[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=tactic-golf "Permalink")

##  Induction on Other Datatypes[🔗](find/?domain=Verso.Genre.Manual.section&name=induction-other-types "Permalink")
Mathematical induction proves a statement for natural numbers by providing a base case for `[Nat.zero](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat.zero in Lean Language Reference")` and an induction step for `[Nat.succ](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat.succ in Lean Language Reference")`. The principle of induction is also valid for other datatypes. Constructors without recursive arguments form the base cases, while constructors with recursive arguments form the induction steps. The ability to carry out proofs by induction is the very reason why they are called _inductive_ datatypes.
One example of this is induction on binary trees. Induction on binary trees is a proof technique where a statement is proven for _all_ binary trees in two steps:
  1. The statement is shown to hold for `[BinTree.leaf](Overloading-and-Type-Classes/Standard-Classes/#BinTree___leaf "Definition of example")`. This is called the base case.
  2. Under the assumption that the statement holds for some arbitrarily chosen trees `l` and `r`, it is shown to hold for `[BinTree.branch](Overloading-and-Type-Classes/Standard-Classes/#BinTree___branch "Definition of example") l x r`, where `x` is an arbitrarily-chosen new data point. This is called the _induction step_. The assumptions that the statement holds for `l` and `r` are called the _induction hypotheses_.


`BinTree.count` counts the number of branches in a tree:
`def BinTree.count : [BinTree](Overloading-and-Type-Classes/Standard-Classes/#BinTree "Definition of example") α → [Nat](https://lean-lang.org/doc/reference/4.26.0/Basic-Types/Natural-Numbers/#Nat___zero "Documentation for Nat in Lean Language Reference")   | [.leaf](Overloading-and-Type-Classes/Standard-Classes/#BinTree___leaf "Definition of example") => 0   | [.branch](Overloading-and-Type-Classes/Standard-Classes/#BinTree___branch "Definition of example") l _ r =>     1 + l.count + r.count`
